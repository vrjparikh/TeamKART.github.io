Transmission
8 Aug 2015
An introduction to the Transmission team, for the freshers of 2015-16

In the previous writeup, we explained in the simplest way possible how the combustion of fuel-air in cylinders is converted to rotation at the crankshaft. The transmission system allows transfer of this energy to the wheels in the most efficient way possible.

The crankshaft may have several parts attached to it, drawing power from it. These include Alternator which is an AC Generator which produces electric current for the on-board electronics and an optional supercharger which compresses air going into the intake to allow the engine to produce more power. Majority of the power generated is utilised to propel the vehicle forward via the transmission.

Transmission or the drivetrain begins with the clutch. It is a set of plates that couples the gearbox and the engine transferring power from the latter to the former. When the clutch is depressed the plates separate and the engine and gearbox become disconnected.

Up next, is the gearbox. The gearbox is one of the most complicated mechanical components in an IC engine car, maybe even more than the engine itself. The image at the top of this blog is the cut away view of the 7-speed S-tronic gearbox from the Audi R8. The sheer number of components and the manufacturing dexterity required makes design and manufacture of gearboxes a huge challenge. Some German made gearboxes may look like they came straight from the forges of Vulcan himself. Only very experienced mechanics usually meddle with those.

Nevertheless, we will make an attempt to explain what it does.

The engine output is a P amount of power, available at the crankshaft, usually a function of engine rpm.

If the engine is set at a certain rpm, Power can be taken as nearly constant.

Power=Torque*Angular velocity

What the gearbox does is varies the ratio of τ and ω. This is according to driving conditions. Lower gears give you a lot of τ (hence more acceleration) but less speed. Higher gears give more speed but less acceleration (for a set rpm). This is achieved using gear ratios. If your vehicle is climbing a slope, higher torque demand, so we shift to a lower gear. For highway use, plane road at a constant speed, shift to a higher gear. An automatic gearbox does this without driver intervention by any torque sensing mechanism.

This is important because engines have an optimum rpm range, called the Power band. We need to utilise available power a efficiently as possible, while keeping the engine within the power band.

The output of the gearbox is carried to the differential via either a chain-sprocket, belt or a driveshaft. A differential distributes power among the wheels while allowing them to rotate at different velocities if needed. FSAE cars are generally Rear wheel drive vehicles.

This video is probably the simplest tutorial for understanding the basic principles of a differential

Did I mention that a powertrain has four kind of power output values?

  • Indicated horsepower: Theoretical. Neglect all real world imperfections.
  • Brake horsepower: Power output measured at the crankshaft. Includes all internal losses due to the engine.
  • Shaft horsepower: Measured at the gearbox output shaft. Indicates power loss across the gearbox.
  • Effective horsepower: Measured at the wheels. Net available power.

The goal of the Transmission team is to achieve maximum efficiency. A light-weight Differential is chosen from many available options. Presently, we use a custom limited slip differential from Drexler Motosport Australia. The KTM Duke engine comes with a built in gearbox and clutch. Remaining components are gear shifting mechanism, the chain linkage and sprocket, differential mount, chain tensioner, driveshafts etc. These components are designed in CAD software, simulated to ensure strength requirements and then put through optimisation algorithms to reduce weight and cost. CNC manufacturing technology is employed to ensure precision wherever possible.
